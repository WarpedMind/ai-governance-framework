## The Three-Horizon Model

AI transformation doesn't happen in a single initiative. Organizations that try to leap to full transformation without building the foundation consistently fail. This roadmap structures the journey across three horizons, each building on the last.

| Horizon | Focus | Typical Timeframe |
|---|---|---|
| **Horizon 1** | Quick wins — automate manual, measurable tasks | Months 1–6 |
| **Horizon 2** | Integration — embed AI into core workflows and products | Months 4–18 |
| **Horizon 3** | Transformation — AI-native operations and competitive differentiation | Month 12+ (ongoing) |

Horizons overlap by design. Horizon 2 planning begins while Horizon 1 is still delivering. Horizon 3 is a destination, not a project.

---

## Horizon 1: Quick Wins (Months 1–6)

### Objective
Demonstrate measurable ROI from AI quickly. Build internal confidence. Identify where AI works in your specific context before scaling.

### Characteristics of good Horizon 1 candidates
- High volume, repetitive, time-consuming tasks
- Clear before/after measurement (time saved, error rate, cost)
- Low risk if the AI output is imperfect
- Does not require significant data infrastructure investment

### Common Horizon 1 use cases
- Document drafting, summarization, and editing
- Customer support triage and FAQ automation
- Internal search and knowledge retrieval
- Code review assistance and boilerplate generation
- Data entry and report generation automation
- Meeting summarization and action item extraction

### Horizon 1 deliverables
- [ ] AI use case inventory (prioritized by effort vs. impact)
- [ ] 2–3 pilot deployments with defined success metrics
- [ ] Baseline measurements captured before deployment
- [ ] Results documented at 30/60/90 days
- [ ] Employee feedback collected
- [ ] AI acceptable use policy in place (see [Governance Policy Template](./ai-governance-policy-template.md))

### Stakeholder alignment
| Stakeholder | What they need to see |
|---|---|
| Executive leadership | ROI evidence; risk is managed |
| Department heads | Their team's workload actually improves |
| Employees | They are augmented, not replaced |
| IT/Security | Tools are vetted and data is protected |

### Horizon 1 KPIs
- Time saved per task (hours/week)
- Error or rework rate before vs. after
- Employee satisfaction with AI tools (survey)
- Cost per output before vs. after
- Number of employees actively using AI tools

## Horizon 2: Integration (Months 4–18)

### Objective
Move AI from standalone tools to embedded capabilities within core workflows, products, and systems. Begin building proprietary data advantages.

### Characteristics of Horizon 2 work
- AI becomes part of the product or operational system, not a separate step
- Requires integration with existing data sources and systems
- Involves more stakeholders and change management
- Begins to create sustainable competitive differentiation

### Common Horizon 2 use cases
- AI embedded in customer-facing products (personalization, recommendations, search)
- Predictive analytics integrated into operational decision-making
- Automated workflows replacing multi-step manual processes end-to-end
- AI-assisted sales, marketing, or customer success tooling
- Internal knowledge systems trained on proprietary organizational data

### Horizon 2 deliverables
- [ ] Data infrastructure assessment and remediation plan
- [ ] AI integration architecture documented
- [ ] Data governance policies updated to reflect AI data flows
- [ ] Model monitoring and drift detection in place
- [ ] Cross-functional AI working group established
- [ ] Updated AI governance policy covering integrated systems
- [ ] Employee training program for AI-augmented roles

### Change management considerations
Horizon 2 is where organizations most often stall. The technology is rarely the limiting factor — culture, change resistance, and unclear ownership are. Plan for:
- Explicit communication about what AI will and will not do to roles
- Retraining pathways for employees whose workflows change significantly
- Clear escalation paths when AI outputs are wrong or unexpected
- Regular all-hands updates on AI progress and direction

### Horizon 2 KPIs
- AI-influenced revenue or pipeline (if customer-facing)
- Process cycle time reduction
- Data quality improvement metrics
- Model accuracy and drift rates
- Employee AI proficiency scores
- AI system uptime and reliability

---

## Horizon 3: Transformation (Month 12+, Ongoing)

### Objective
AI is no longer a layer on top of the business — it is embedded in how the organization operates, competes, and grows. New capabilities become possible that were not previously viable.

### Characteristics of Horizon 3
- AI-native processes designed from the ground up, not retrofitted
- Autonomous or semi-autonomous systems operating with defined governance guardrails
- Proprietary data and model advantages are a real competitive moat
- Organization has AI literacy across all levels, not just technical teams
- Continuous improvement loops are built into AI systems

### Common Horizon 3 capabilities
- Autonomous data workflows that adapt based on outcomes
- Predictive systems that surface opportunities before humans identify them
- AI agents handling complex, multi-step operational tasks end-to-end
- Real-time personalization at scale
- AI-augmented strategic planning and scenario modeling

### Horizon 3 is not a destination — it is a posture
Organizations in Horizon 3 are not "done with AI transformation." They are continuously iterating, monitoring, and evolving. The governance infrastructure built in Horizons 1 and 2 becomes critical here — without it, autonomous systems create compounding risk.

### Horizon 3 KPIs
- AI-driven revenue as a percentage of total revenue
- Autonomous process coverage (% of workflows with AI-in-the-loop)
- Time from insight to action (strategic responsiveness)
- AI system ROI across the portfolio
- Regulatory compliance posture (especially as AI regulation matures)

## Cross-Horizon Principles

These apply throughout all three horizons:

**Start with the problem, not the technology.**  
Every AI initiative should begin with a clearly defined business problem and measurable success criteria. "We should use AI" is not a strategy.

**Governance first, scale second.**  
It is far cheaper to build governance structures before scaling than to retrofit them after an incident. See the [AI Governance Policy Template](./ai-governance-policy-template.md).

**Measure everything.**  
AI initiatives that cannot demonstrate measurable impact lose organizational support. Define metrics before deployment, not after.

**Expect iteration.**  
First deployments rarely look like final deployments. Build feedback loops and plan for revision cycles.

**People are the hardest part.**  
Technology adoption is a people problem as much as a technology problem. Change management investment is not optional.
